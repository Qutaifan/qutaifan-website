Buyer's Guide

How to Choose the Right Contractor Insurance

Before you spend a dollar, understand what you're actually buying — and what you're not.

1

Know your required liability limits before you shop

Most commercial clients and GCs require a minimum of $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate for general liability. Government contracts often require $2M per occurrence. Check your contract requirements before comparing quotes — buying a $500K policy and then needing to upgrade mid-project is expensive and disruptive. Get the limits right first, then find the best price for those limits.

2

Understand what general liability actually covers

General liability (GL) covers third-party bodily injury and property damage caused by your work — a client trips over your equipment, you break a pipe during a renovation, your work causes water damage. It does NOT cover your own injuries (that's workers' comp), your tools and equipment (separate policy), professional mistakes or bad advice (professional liability / E&O), or your employees' injuries. Understand the gaps before assuming you're covered.

3

Always get a certificate of insurance (COI) ready

A certificate of insurance is the document your clients and general contractors will demand before you set foot on a job site. It proves your coverage is active and lists the policy limits. The speed at which you can produce a COI is a real competitive advantage — contractors who can email a COI link in 60 seconds win jobs over those who say "I'll get it to you by end of week." Next Insurance and Thimble both enable instant digital COIs.

4

Know when you need more than GL

If you have employees: you need workers' compensation (required by law in most states). If you give professional advice alongside physical work: you need professional liability (E&O). If you drive for work: commercial auto (personal auto policies typically exclude business use). If you have a shop or warehouse: commercial property or a BOP. Most full-time contractors with employees need at minimum GL + workers' comp + commercial auto — not just GL alone.

5

Occurrence vs. claims-made: know which you're buying

Occurrence policies cover incidents that happened during the policy period, even if the claim is filed years later — the standard and typically better form for contractors. Claims-made policies only cover claims filed while the policy is active; if you cancel the policy and a client sues two years later for prior work, you're unprotected unless you buy "tail" coverage. Most GL policies for contractors are occurrence-based, but always confirm before purchasing.

6

Consider a BOP to bundle and save

A Business Owner's Policy (BOP) combines general liability and commercial property insurance into a single bundled premium, typically at 10–25% less than buying each separately. If you have business property to protect — equipment, tools stored at a shop, an office — a BOP from The Hartford or Next Insurance often beats the cost of two separate policies. Not all contractors need property coverage, but those who do should price a BOP before buying GL alone.

FAQ

Contractor Insurance Questions, Answered Honestly

Do contractors actually need business insurance, or can I skip it?

You need it — for two separate reasons. First, it's often legally or contractually required: most states mandate workers' comp for contractors with employees, and virtually every commercial client will demand a GL certificate before you start work. Second, an uninsured contractor who causes property damage or injury faces personal liability — your personal assets are at risk, not just your business. A single lawsuit without coverage can cost $50,000–$500,000+. At $25–$75/month for quality GL coverage, the math is not close. The only contractors who can credibly skip it are hobbyists who never work for pay.

How much does contractor insurance typically cost in 2026?

General liability for a solo contractor doing under $150K in annual revenue typically runs $25–$75/month ($300–$900/year) for $1M/$2M limits. The main cost drivers are: your trade (roofing, demolition, and excavation are significantly more expensive than painting or landscaping), your annual revenue, your state, your claims history, and the policy limits you choose. A 3-employee plumbing company with $500K revenue might pay $1,500–$3,000/year for GL + workers' comp combined. Get at least three quotes using the providers above — pricing variance across carriers for the same risk can be 40–60%.

What is a certificate of insurance and why do clients demand it?

A certificate of insurance (COI) is a one-page document from your insurer that summarizes your active coverage — policy type, limits, carrier name, and policy period. Clients and general contractors require it before allowing you on a job site because it proves you have the coverage to pay for damages if something goes wrong. They're protecting themselves: if you cause a $200,000 flood and you're uninsured, they're the ones left holding the bill. A COI doesn't transfer coverage to them — it just proves you have it. Always have your current COI accessible. Providers like Next Insurance and Thimble let you share a live link to your COI in seconds.

What's the difference between general liability and professional liability for contractors?

General liability (GL) covers physical damage and bodily injury — a client gets hurt at your job site, you accidentally damage their property, a product you install causes harm. Professional liability (also called Errors & Omissions or E&O) covers claims that your professional advice, design, or consulting work caused a financial loss — a design you specified was wrong, a project plan you created led to cost overruns, you gave advice that the client relied on and it was incorrect. Physical trade contractors (electricians, plumbers, carpenters) primarily need GL. Contractors who also advise, design, or consult — design-build firms, IT subcontractors, engineering contractors — need both. Hiscox and The Hartford both offer both in one policy.

Can I get contractor insurance the same day I need it?

Yes — with the right provider. Next Insurance and Thimble both offer same-day (often same-hour) coverage activation and instant digital certificates of insurance. Quote, purchase, and have a COI link ready to send in under 10 minutes. Simply Business and biBERK are also fast but may take a few hours to bind. The Hartford and Travelers, being agent-based, typically take 24–72 hours for a full policy to bind. If you're starting a job tomorrow and just realized you don't have coverage, Next Insurance or Thimble are your fastest paths. If your client needs you added as an "additional insured" on the certificate, confirm the provider can do that — most digital-first providers support it.
