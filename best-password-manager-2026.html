LastPass — Still Functional, With Caveats

★★★½ Proceed with Caution Free · $3.00/mo Premium

LastPass was the dominant password manager for a decade. In 2022 and 2023, a series of serious security breaches changed the conversation permanently. Encrypted vault data was exfiltrated — and while the zero-knowledge encryption means passwords remained protected, the company's response, disclosure timeline, and security posture were widely criticized by the security community. If you're a current LastPass user with a strong master password, your passwords are likely still secure. But we can no longer recommend it as a first choice for new users.

What LastPass does well: the interface is familiar and clean, the security dashboard highlights weak and reused passwords clearly, and the emergency access feature (designating a trusted contact who can access your vault after a waiting period) is one of the better implementations of this feature in the market. For existing users who understand the context and have taken appropriate steps (strong master password, 2FA), it remains a functional tool. For new users, start with 1Password or Bitwarden instead.

Buyer's Guide

How to Choose the Right Password Manager

Before you commit, understand the six features that actually matter for your security and peace of mind.

1

Zero-knowledge encryption is non-negotiable

The company should never be able to read your passwords — not even in theory. Zero-knowledge architecture means your master password never leaves your device; only encrypted vault data is transmitted. Every manager on this list claims zero-knowledge. Ask for the security whitepaper and independent audits before trusting that claim. Bitwarden and 1Password publish full audit reports publicly.

2

Two-factor authentication support

Your vault is only as secure as your master password alone if you skip 2FA. Every manager worth using supports TOTP authenticators (Google Authenticator, Authy) at minimum. Premium tiers typically add hardware key support (YubiKey) — essential for high-value accounts. Enable 2FA on your password manager before storing your first credential.

3

Cross-platform consistency

A password manager you only use on one device is a password manager you won't use consistently. Before committing, verify native apps exist for every device you use — iPhone, Android, Windows, Mac, Linux — and test the browser extension in your primary browser. A single weak platform will break the habit. 1Password and Bitwarden have the most consistent cross-platform experience.

4

Emergency access for trusted contacts

If you become incapacitated or pass away, can someone you trust access your accounts? Emergency access (available in 1Password, LastPass, Keeper, and RoboForm) lets you designate a trusted contact who can request vault access after a configurable waiting period — giving you time to deny the request if you're available. This is an underrated estate planning feature that most people overlook until it's too late.

5

Breach monitoring and password health

A passive credential check — done once when you set up the manager — is not enough. The best managers (1Password Watchtower, Keeper BreachWatch, Dashlane's dark web monitor) continuously watch breach databases and alert you the moment a credential appears. Look for proactive, real-time monitoring rather than manual-scan-only features. This is the difference between knowing immediately and finding out six months later.

6

Migration and data portability

Before you invest hours importing passwords into a new manager, verify you can export them in a standard format (CSV, JSON, 1PIF) if you ever need to switch. Managers that lock your data in proprietary formats create vendor lock-in risk. Bitwarden and 1Password both export clean, portable files. Also check whether your new manager can import directly from your current one — most support common formats from LastPass, Chrome, and Firefox.

FAQ

Common Questions, Answered Honestly

Are password managers actually safe to use in 2026?

Yes — for the vast majority of users, a reputable password manager is orders of magnitude safer than the alternative (reusing passwords or writing them in a document). The risk is not using one. Zero-knowledge encryption means that even if a company is breached, your passwords remain encrypted with a key only you hold. The LastPass incident in 2022 is the most cited counterexample — and even then, users with strong, unique master passwords and 2FA were protected. Stick to audited, zero-knowledge managers like 1Password, Bitwarden, or Keeper and your risk surface shrinks dramatically.

What happens to my passwords if the company shuts down?

This is a legitimate concern. The best managers address it directly: 1Password maintains an "Emergency Kit" — a printable document with your account credentials and Secret Key — for exactly this scenario. Bitwarden lets you self-host your own server so company shutdown is irrelevant. Enpass stores data locally with no cloud dependency at all. For any cloud-based manager, regularly export your vault in a portable format (CSV or JSON) and keep a secure offline copy. This takes five minutes and protects you against any company-level risk.

Why not just use the password manager built into Chrome or Safari?

Browser-based password managers have improved significantly, but they still have meaningful gaps: they typically only work within that browser (Chrome passwords don't sync to Safari on iPhone), they lack zero-knowledge guarantees (Google and Apple can technically access your data under certain legal conditions), and they offer none of the advanced features — breach monitoring, secure sharing, emergency access, travel mode, encrypted notes, or 2FA storage. For low-stakes personal use, browser managers are acceptable. For anything involving financial accounts, work credentials, or shared access, a dedicated manager is worth the $2–5/month premium.

Is one master password really enough to protect everything?

A strong master password — 16+ characters, unique, never used elsewhere — combined with hardware 2FA (a YubiKey) is exceptionally difficult to crack through any realistic attack vector. The attack surface is your master password being guessed or phished, and your 2FA being intercepted. To minimize both: use a passphrase (four random words are easier to remember and harder to crack than a complex string), enable 2FA with a hardware key rather than SMS, and never type your master password on untrusted devices. The math on AES-256 encryption with a strong master password is not the weak link — human behavior is.

1Password vs Bitwarden — which should I actually choose?

If you want the most polished experience and don't mind paying $2.99/month, choose 1Password. The UX is noticeably better, Travel Mode is unique, and the family plan is excellent value. If you want maximum transparency, zero cost, or self-hosting capability, choose Bitwarden. Both use genuine zero-knowledge architecture, both have clean independent security audits, and both will protect your credentials effectively. The decision comes down to: how much do you value a premium interface vs. open-source auditability and free pricing? For most people, 1Password wins on daily usability; for security researchers and privacy advocates, Bitwarden wins on principle.
