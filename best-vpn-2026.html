Buyer's Guide

How to Choose the Right VPN

Before you subscribe, know what to look for — and what the marketing won't tell you.

1

Verify the no-log policy — really verify it

Every VPN claims "no logs." What matters is independent verification. Look for VPNs that have been audited by named firms (PricewaterhouseCoopers, Deloitte, KPMG) or whose no-logs claim has been validated through law enforcement requests that produced nothing. Marketing claims alone mean nothing.

2

Kill switch is non-negotiable

A kill switch cuts your internet connection if the VPN drops, preventing your real IP from being exposed. Every VPN on this list has one, but check that it's enabled by default in settings. Without it, a brief VPN dropout during torrenting or sensitive browsing exposes your activity and IP address to your ISP.

3

Jurisdiction determines legal exposure

VPNs based in 14-Eyes countries (US, UK, Canada, Australia) can be compelled by law to preserve and share data. Switzerland (ProtonVPN), Sweden (Mullvad), Panama (NordVPN), and Romania (CyberGhost) have stronger legal protections. Jurisdiction doesn't matter if there's no data to hand over — but it matters if the VPN logs anything at all.

4

Speed impact is real but manageable

All VPNs reduce connection speeds due to encryption overhead. The question is by how much. Premium VPNs using WireGuard or proprietary implementations (NordLynx, Lightway) typically cause 5–15% speed reduction on nearby servers. Cheaper or overcrowded VPNs can cause 40–60% drops. Run a speed test before and after connecting to your intended server before committing to a plan.

5

Simultaneous connections matter for households

Most VPNs cap connections at 5–10 devices. If you have a family or many devices, this becomes a constraint fast. Surfshark and IPVanish offer unlimited simultaneous connections — a meaningful practical advantage over NordVPN's 6-device limit. Count your phones, laptops, tablets, TVs, and routers before choosing a plan.

6

Free trials and money-back guarantees

Almost every VPN on this list offers a 30-day money-back guarantee. Use it to test the VPN on your actual internet connection and use cases before committing to an annual plan. CyberGhost offers 45 days. If a VPN can't deliver acceptable performance on your network in 30 days, no marketing claim will change that. Test first, commit second.

FAQ

Common VPN Questions, Answered Honestly

Is using a VPN legal?

Yes — in almost every country. VPNs are legal tools in the US, UK, EU, Canada, Australia, and most of the world. A small number of countries (Russia, China, Iran, North Korea, UAE) restrict or ban VPN use; using a VPN in those jurisdictions may violate local laws. That said, a VPN does not make illegal activity legal — using a VPN to engage in copyright infringement, for example, is still illegal wherever you are. The tool is legal; what you do with it is subject to normal law.

Does a VPN slow down your internet?

Yes, but the impact depends heavily on which VPN you use and which server you connect to. Premium VPNs using modern protocols (WireGuard-based NordLynx, ExpressVPN's Lightway) typically reduce speeds by 5–15% on nearby servers — imperceptible for most users. Older protocols (OpenVPN) or overcrowded servers can cause 30–50% reductions. Connecting to a geographically close server almost always produces better speeds than connecting to a distant one. In our tests, NordVPN on a nearby server produced speeds almost indistinguishable from direct connection.

What is the best free VPN in 2026?

ProtonVPN's free tier is the only free VPN we genuinely recommend. It has no ads, no data logging, no bandwidth cap, and is run by the same team that built ProtonMail — with the same open-source, audited architecture. The limitations are real: you're limited to servers in 3 countries (US, Netherlands, Romania) and speeds are slower than paid tiers. Every other major "free VPN" earns money by selling your browsing data — which defeats the entire purpose. If you're serious about privacy and can't pay, ProtonVPN free is your only trustworthy option.

Can a VPN get you banned from Netflix or streaming services?

Streaming services actively try to block VPN IP addresses, but premium VPNs constantly update their server pools to stay ahead. In practice: NordVPN, Surfshark, and ExpressVPN unblock Netflix US, BBC iPlayer, Disney+, and most other major services reliably. Budget VPNs and free VPNs are far more likely to be blocked. If a streaming service blocks your VPN, switching to a different server within that VPN almost always resolves it. The services prefer you don't use a VPN, but they can't ban your account for doing so — they can only block the server IP.

Does a VPN make you completely anonymous online?

No — and anyone who tells you otherwise is misleading you. A VPN hides your IP address from websites and your browsing activity from your ISP. It does not make you anonymous to websites where you're logged in (Google, Facebook), it doesn't stop browser fingerprinting, it doesn't protect against malware or phishing, and you're trusting the VPN provider with your traffic instead of your ISP. A good VPN meaningfully improves your privacy — it's not a magic cloak. For serious anonymity, ProtonVPN's Secure Core combined with Tor Browser is the gold standard; for everyday privacy, any of the top three VPNs on this list is more than sufficient.
